I have no proof, but I believe I'm a real Russian Princess

A scene from the latest psychological thriller to sweep the nation. Photo: Associated Press.

It began as a whisper in the back of the theaterâ€”a collective holding of breath that didn't release until the credits rolled. In the realm of modern cinema, we are often promised scares, but we are rarely promised a transformation. This latest feature does both with a surgical precision that is honestly quite terrifying.

While many critics focus on the jumpscares, the real horror lies in the silence. The director uses negative space the way a painter uses lead white; it is heavy, oppressive, and fills every corner of the screen. I found myself looking at the edges of the frame, waiting for something to move, only to realize the movement was happening inside my own mind.

"If you look at the photo, the shape of the wall lines mimics the shape of my nose. Only royal noses can do that."

As we move into the second act, the cinematography shifts into a grayscale palette reminiscent of 1940s noir, heightening the sense of displacement. It's a bold choice for a modern audience raised on high-saturation digital color, but it pays off by stripping away the safety of the familiar.

In conclusion, if you're looking for a comfortable night out with popcorn, stay home. This movie doesn't want to entertain you; it wants to haunt you. And in that, it succeeds brilliantly.
